## World and Politics

The biggest story overnight is Hungary. Voters turned out in record numbers, the highest since the nineteen nineties, and ended Viktor Orban's sixteen-year grip on power. His Fidesz party was swept aside in a decisive rejection of right-wing populism that's being felt across Europe. It's a major political shift for a country that has been one of the EU's most persistent outliers.

On the Middle East, the US-Iran ceasefire is technically holding, but the situation is escalating in other ways. After peace talks in Islamabad collapsed over the weekend with no deal, President Trump announced the US Navy will blockade Iranian port traffic and interdict every ship that has paid a toll to Tehran. He also criticized Pope Leo the Fourteenth for his stance on the conflict, saying quote, we don't like a pope that's going to say that it's okay to have a nuclear weapon. Pakistan has deployed F-sixteen fighter jets to Saudi Arabia under their joint defense agreement as Iranian missiles continue to target Gulf states. The casualty toll from the broader conflict now stands at over two thousand dead in Iran, at least twenty-six in Israel, and dozens more across Gulf states.

Pope Leo the Fourteenth, meanwhile, is beginning an eleven-day trip to Africa today, starting in Algiers.

## Markets and Business

Markets had their best week since November, with the S and P five hundred up three point six percent, the Nasdaq up nearly five percent, and the Dow adding three percent. But those gains are under pressure heading into Monday. Futures are falling on Trump's blockade announcement, and oil is surging again. West Texas Intermediate jumped eight and a half percent to nearly one hundred and five dollars a barrel. Brent crude is back above one hundred and two dollars.

This is a big week for earnings. Goldman Sachs reports Monday, followed by JPMorgan Chase, Citigroup, Wells Fargo, Morgan Stanley, and Bank of America through the rest of the week. Bank earnings will tell us a lot about how the Iran conflict and oil shock are flowing through to the real economy.

The IMF and World Bank are also meeting in Washington this week, with the Iran crisis looming over every conversation about global growth.

## Technology

A new study from PwC shows that just twenty percent of companies are capturing three-quarters of AI's economic gains. The leading firms aren't just using AI for cost-cutting. They're two to three times more likely to use it for growth and business model reinvention. The gap between AI leaders and everyone else is widening fast.

In infrastructure news, Broadcom signed a long-term deal with Google to develop custom tensor processing units, and separately, Anthropic announced that Broadcom will supply TPU compute capacity for its Claude platform. Anthropic's Model Context Protocol also crossed ninety-seven million installs in March, making it the default way AI agents connect to external tools across the industry.

And a team at UC San Diego published a new chip design that could make data centers significantly more energy-efficient by rethinking how power is converted for GPUs. If it works at scale, it could meaningfully reduce the electricity demands of AI training.
