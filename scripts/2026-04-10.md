It's Friday, April tenth, twenty twenty-six. Good morning. Here's what's happening today.

## World and Politics

High-stakes diplomacy begins tomorrow in Islamabad. The US delegation, led by Vice President JD Vance along with special envoy Steve Witkoff and Jared Kushner, is heading to Pakistan for talks aimed at converting the shaky two-week ceasefire with Iran into something more durable. Iran is expected to be represented by the speaker of its parliament, Mohammad Bagher Ghalibaf, and has signaled it will push a ten-point plan as the basis for negotiations. The ceasefire itself is fragile. Israel conducted its largest strike yet on Beirut Wednesday, one hundred hits in ten minutes, killing the nephew of a Hezbollah leader. Netanyahu said yesterday there is no ceasefire in Lebanon and vowed to keep fighting Hezbollah, though Israel will begin direct talks with the Lebanese government. Iran called the Lebanon strikes a grave violation and warned of strong responses. Preliminary casualty figures from the five-week war stand at over two thousand in Iran, twenty-six in Israel, and dozens more across the Gulf.

## Markets and Business

Stocks extended their relief rally yesterday despite the ceasefire turbulence. The Dow rose more than two hundred fifty points to close at forty-eight thousand one hundred fifty, its best two-day performance since last April. The S and P five hundred added six tenths of a percent to finish at six thousand eight hundred twenty-four, and the Nasdaq gained eight tenths. The Dow is now positive for twenty twenty-six for the first time. Oil stayed volatile. West Texas Intermediate briefly pushed back above one hundred dollars before settling at ninety-seven eighty-seven, up more than three percent on the day. Only five ships crossed the Strait of Hormuz on the first day of the ceasefire, all bulk carriers, no oil or gas tankers yet, which is keeping energy markets tense. This morning, futures are pulling back about four tenths of a percent across the board as traders weigh whether Saturday's Islamabad talks will actually deliver something or collapse.

## Technology

Google launched Gemini three point one Flash-Lite, a new efficiency-focused model promising two and a half times faster response times and forty-five percent faster output at just twenty-five cents per million input tokens. That's cheap enough to run at real scale. Anthropic's Model Context Protocol crossed ninety-seven million installs in March. MCP started as an experimental standard for connecting AI models to tools and data, and it is now quietly becoming foundational infrastructure for building AI agents. Meanwhile, Australian software company Atlassian announced it is laying off roughly sixteen hundred employees, about ten percent of its global workforce, to redirect resources toward AI development. It is the latest in a growing list of major tech firms restructuring around AI. And OpenAI has now crossed twenty-five billion dollars in annualized revenue and is reportedly taking early steps toward an IPO, potentially later this year. Anthropic is not far behind at around nineteen billion.

## One More Thing

Twelve major airlines have issued new advice for passengers flying through Dubai International Airport this month because of regional tensions. Dubai is one of the busiest connecting airports on the planet, handling nearly one hundred million passengers a year, and when its airspace gets complicated, global travel ripples in unexpected directions.
