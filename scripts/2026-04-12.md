## World and Politics

The big story this week remains the US-Iran standoff, and where things go from here is still very much an open question. Peace talks in Islamabad wrapped up after twenty-one hours with no deal. Vice President Vance said the conversations were intense and constructive, but that Iran refused to make a fundamental commitment to abandon its nuclear program. Meanwhile, President Trump is escalating pressure on the Strait of Hormuz, ordering the Navy to interdict vessels that have paid tolls to Iran and to begin clearing mines the Iranians laid in the strait. The waterway remains a flashpoint for global energy markets.

Elsewhere, Hungary is holding parliamentary elections today, a closely watched vote across Europe. Ukraine and Russia are exchanging prisoners ahead of Orthodox Easter, a small but meaningful gesture during a long conflict. And Pope Leo the Fourteenth used his Saturday address to call on world leaders to reject war as a means of settling disputes.

## Markets and Business

Markets posted their best week since November, powered by the US-Iran ceasefire announced earlier in the week. The S and P five hundred gained about three point six percent for the week, the Nasdaq rose nearly five percent, and the Dow added three percent. Friday was quieter, with the S and P slipping one tenth of a percent to close at sixty-eight sixteen.

The ceasefire rally was dramatic. On the day it was announced, the Dow surged over thirteen hundred points. Oil was the mirror image. West Texas Intermediate crude crashed more than sixteen percent in a single session, settling at ninety-four dollars a barrel, its biggest daily drop since April twenty twenty. That's good news for anyone running a business with energy costs, though the ceasefire is only two weeks long and the underlying tensions haven't been resolved.

One area to watch: software stocks sold off on Friday amid growing concern that AI is disrupting the traditional software-as-a-service business model. That theme isn't going away.

## Technology

Meta unveiled Muse Spark, its first major AI model since bringing in Scale AI's Alexandr Wang to lead Meta Superintelligence Labs. The model is part of Meta's push to close the gap with OpenAI and Google. Speaking of OpenAI, they've crossed twenty-five billion dollars in annualized revenue and are reportedly taking early steps toward a public listing, possibly as soon as late this year. Anthropic is not far behind at nineteen billion.

Google released Gemini three point one Flash-Lite, which delivers two and a half times faster response times at just twenty-five cents per million input tokens. And a research team published a breakthrough approach that could cut AI energy consumption by up to one hundred times while actually improving accuracy, by combining neural networks with symbolic reasoning. If that scales, it could reshape the economics of the entire industry.

## One More Thing

Japan is deploying robots to fill jobs that nobody wants. The country's Ministry of Economy is building a domestic physical AI sector, aiming to capture thirty percent of the global market by twenty forty. These aren't science fiction androids. They're practical machines working in warehouses, farms, and factories, doing the repetitive and physically demanding work that Japan's aging population can no longer fill. It's one of the most concrete examples yet of AI solving a real demographic problem, not replacing workers, but showing up where there simply aren't enough people.
