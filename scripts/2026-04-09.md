It's Thursday, April ninth, twenty twenty-six. Good morning. Here's what's happening today.

## World and Politics

The fragile two-week ceasefire between the United States, Israel, and Iran is already under severe strain. While the deal brokered through Pakistani mediation paused five weeks of direct conflict, Israel launched a massive wave of strikes across Lebanon yesterday targeting Hezbollah, killing at least two hundred people and injuring over a thousand. Lebanon has declared a national day of mourning. Israel insists Lebanon is not covered by the ceasefire agreement, but Iran views the attacks as a provocation that could unravel the entire deal. Meanwhile, several Gulf states including the UAE, Saudi Arabia, and Kuwait reported missile and drone attacks on their territory even after the truce was announced, with the UAE saying its air defenses were actively engaging incoming fire. The question now is whether this ceasefire holds through the week, or whether the region slides back into open conflict.

## Markets and Business

Markets surged on the ceasefire news yesterday in one of the biggest relief rallies of the year. The Dow jumped over thirteen hundred points, closing up nearly three percent at forty-seven thousand nine hundred ten. The S and P five hundred gained two and a half percent, and the Nasdaq rose nearly three percent. Oil prices cratered. West Texas Intermediate crude fell more than sixteen percent to ninety-four dollars a barrel, its biggest single-day drop since April twenty twenty. Airlines were the standout winners, with Delta up twelve percent and American Airlines up eleven percent on the plunging fuel costs. This morning, futures are pulling back slightly, down about three tenths of a percent across the board. Traders are watching closely to see if the ceasefire actually holds. If it does, the reopening of shipping lanes through the Strait of Hormuz would relieve enormous pressure on global energy prices. If it doesn't, expect yesterday's gains to reverse fast.

## Technology

Meta unveiled Muse Spark, the first model from its new Muse series developed by Meta Superintelligence Labs. The model will roll out across Facebook, Instagram, WhatsApp, and Messenger, as well as on Ray-Ban Meta AI glasses. Meta also confirmed its AI capital expenditures this year will be between one hundred fifteen and one hundred thirty-five billion dollars, nearly double last year. In other AI news, Anthropic released Claude Mythos Preview, a cybersecurity-focused model that has already discovered thousands of previously unknown zero-day vulnerabilities. It's being deployed through a limited partnership program with over forty companies including Microsoft, Amazon, Apple, and Google for defensive security only. And researchers published a breakthrough in AI energy efficiency, combining neural networks with symbolic reasoning to cut energy use by up to one hundred times while actually improving accuracy. If that scales, it could fundamentally change the economics of running large models.

## One More Thing

Scientists just confirmed that Saturn's magnetic field is lopsided. Unlike Earth's relatively symmetrical magnetic shield, Saturn's field is noticeably skewed and off-center. Nobody is entirely sure why, which makes it one of those good planetary mysteries worth following.
