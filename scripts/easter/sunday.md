# Easter Sunday

Francisco, Sebastian — it is Sunday morning. And the story turns.

It is still dark when Mary Magdalene goes to the tomb. The Gospel of John is very specific about this — it is early, before dawn, and she goes alone. She has been waiting since Friday to do what she could not do before the Sabbath: care for the body, anoint it with spices, say a proper goodbye.

She arrives and the stone has been moved.

She does not go in. She runs. She finds Peter and John and tells them, "They have taken the Lord from the tomb, and we do not know where they have laid him."

Peter and John run. John is faster — the Gospel actually notes this — but he stops at the entrance. Peter, being Peter, goes straight in. The burial cloths are there, folded neatly. The cloth that had covered his face is rolled up, set aside in a separate place. The body is gone.

John enters. He sees. And he believes.

They go home. But Mary stays. She stands outside the tomb, weeping. She looks inside and sees two figures in white sitting where the body had been, one at the head and one at the feet. They ask her, "Woman, why are you weeping?"

She turns around and sees a man standing there. She thinks he is the gardener. He says one word to her.

"Mary."

And she knows.

This is the moment. Not a thunderclap, not an army, not a cosmic display of power. A woman standing in a garden at dawn, and someone says her name. That is how the resurrection enters the world — quietly, personally, in a voice she recognizes.

She reaches for him. He says, "Do not hold on to me, for I have not yet ascended to the Father." And then he sends her to tell the others. Mary Magdalene becomes the first person to carry this news: he is alive.

Francisco, Sebastian — Christians call this the most important event in human history. Not because it is dramatic in the way you might expect. There are no special effects. A woman goes to grieve, and instead of a body she finds a conversation. She is asked why she is crying, and then she is called by name.

Think about what this means for the people in the story. Peter, who denied Jesus three times, will see him again. The disciples hiding in a locked room will hear him say, "Peace be with you." Thomas, who refuses to believe without proof, will be invited to touch the wounds. No one is excluded. No one is punished for their doubt or their fear.

The readings for today include a line from the Acts of the Apostles where Peter stands up and says, simply: "We are witnesses." That is all. We saw what we saw. Make of it what you will.

Easter is not really about eggs or chocolate or bunnies. Those are fine traditions, but the center of it is this: the claim that death is not the last word. That love, even when it appears to lose completely — abandoned, mocked, killed, buried — is not finished. The stone is moved. The tomb is empty. The gardener says your name.

Two thousand years later, billions of people around the world wake up this morning and greet each other with words that have been spoken since the earliest Christians gathered in secret, in catacombs, at risk of their own lives:

"Christ is risen."

And the response:

"He is risen indeed."

Happy Easter, Francisco. Happy Easter, Sebastian. This is a story worth knowing.
