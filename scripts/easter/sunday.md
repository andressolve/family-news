# Easter Sunday

Francisco, Sebastian — it is Sunday morning. And the story turns.

On Friday, Jesus was crucified. His body was placed in a tomb carved from rock, and a heavy stone was rolled across the entrance. All day Saturday, his friends sat in grief. The world was still. Nothing moved. Nothing changed.

Until now.

It is still dark when Mary Magdalene makes her way to the tomb. She has been waiting since Friday to do what she could not do before the Sabbath — care for the body, anoint it with spices, say a proper goodbye. She walks alone, before dawn, carrying her oils.

She arrives. And the stone has been moved.

She does not go in. She turns and runs. She finds Peter and John and says, "They have taken the Lord from the tomb, and we do not know where they have laid him."

Peter and John run to see for themselves. John is faster — the Gospel actually mentions this little detail — but he stops at the entrance. Peter, being Peter, goes straight in. The burial cloths are there, folded neatly. The cloth that had covered the face is rolled up, set aside on its own. But the body is gone.

John steps in behind him. He sees the empty tomb. And he believes.

They go home. But Mary stays.

She stands outside the tomb, weeping. She looks inside and sees two figures in white, sitting where the body had been — one at the head and one at the feet. They ask her, "Why are you weeping?"

She turns around. There is a man standing there. She thinks he is the gardener. And then he says one word to her.

"Mary."

And she knows.

This is the moment. Not a thunderclap. Not an army. Not a display of cosmic power. A woman standing in a garden at dawn, and someone says her name. That is how the resurrection enters the world — quietly, personally, in a voice she recognizes.

She reaches for him. He tells her gently, "Do not hold on to me." And then he sends her to tell the others. Mary Magdalene — the same woman who had been weeping a moment before — becomes the first person in history to carry this news: he is alive.

Francisco, Sebastian — think about what this means for the people we have been following through these three days.

Peter, who denied Jesus three times on Friday night, will see him again. Peter is not cast out. He is forgiven.

The disciples, hiding behind locked doors in fear, will hear Jesus walk in and say, "Peace be with you."

Thomas, who refuses to believe without proof, will be invited to touch the wounds with his own hands.

No one is excluded. No one is punished for their doubt, or their fear, or their failure.

Easter is not really about eggs or chocolate or bunnies. Those are fine — enjoy them. But the center of it is this. The claim that death is not the last word. That love, even when it appears to lose completely — abandoned, mocked, killed, buried — is not finished. The stone is moved. The tomb is empty. The gardener says your name.

Two thousand years later, billions of people around the world wake up this morning and greet each other with words that have been spoken since the very first Christians gathered in secret, in catacombs, at risk of their own lives.

"Christ is risen."

And the response — "He is risen indeed."

Happy Easter, Francisco. Happy Easter, Sebastian. This is a story worth knowing.
