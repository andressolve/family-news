# Good Friday

Francisco, Sebastian — today is Good Friday. It is the most solemn day in the Christian calendar. And I want to tell you what happened, because the story itself deserves to be heard.

It begins at night. Jesus has just finished a meal with his closest friends — the Last Supper, you may have heard it called. He knows what is coming. He goes to a garden called Gethsemane, on the Mount of Olives, just outside Jerusalem. He asks his friends to stay awake with him while he prays. They fall asleep. He prays alone.

Then the torches come through the trees. A crowd, sent by the temple authorities, armed with swords and clubs. And Judas — one of his own twelve companions — steps forward and kisses him on the cheek. That is the signal. That is how the soldiers know which man to arrest.

They take him. His friends scatter into the dark.

What follows is a series of trials — first before the high priest, then before Pontius Pilate, the Roman governor. Pilate does not find him guilty of anything. He asks the crowd what they want. The crowd demands crucifixion. Pilate washes his hands and gives the order.

There is a detail in the Gospel of John that has stayed with people for two thousand years. During the trial, Pilate asks Jesus, "What is truth?" And then he walks away without waiting for an answer. Think about that for a moment. He asks the deepest question a person can ask — and does not stay to hear the response.

They lead Jesus through the streets carrying the wooden cross on which he will die. He is taken to a hill called Golgotha — the Place of the Skull — and crucified between two criminals. It is about noon.

His mother, Mary, stands at the foot of the cross. She does not leave.

From the cross, Jesus speaks seven times. He says, "Father, forgive them — they do not know what they are doing." He tells one of the criminals beside him, "Today you will be with me in paradise." He looks at his mother and his friend John and tells them to take care of each other. Near the end, he cries out, "My God, my God, why have you forsaken me?" — which is the opening line of Psalm twenty-two, a prayer written hundreds of years before, by King David.

At about three in the afternoon, he dies. The sky goes dark. The earth shakes. The curtain in the great temple of Jerusalem — a massive veil separating the holiest inner chamber from the rest of the world — tears in two, from top to bottom.

A Roman centurion, a soldier who has watched the whole thing, says: "Truly, this was the Son of God."

They take his body down and lay it in a tomb cut from rock, belonging to a man named Joseph of Arimathea. A heavy stone is rolled across the entrance.

And then — silence.

That is where today sits. In the silence after. The Church strips its altars bare tonight. There is no music, no celebration. The tabernacle is left open and empty. Everything stops.

Francisco, Sebastian — this is a story about the worst thing that can happen. An innocent man, abandoned by his friends, killed by the state, mourned by his mother. Christians have sat with this story every year for two thousand years, not to look away from suffering, but to look directly at it.

Tomorrow, we sit in the stillness. And then — something else happens. But that is for Sunday.

Rest with this tonight. It is enough.
