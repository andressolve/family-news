# Holy Saturday

Francisco, Sebastian — today is Holy Saturday. It is the strangest day of the year. Nothing happens. And that is the whole point.

Yesterday, Jesus died. Tomorrow — well, you will hear tomorrow's story tomorrow. But today, the people who loved him do not know that. They only know that he is gone.

Imagine the disciples. These are ordinary men — fishermen, tax collectors — who left everything to follow someone they believed was sent by God. They watched him heal people, calm storms, speak with an authority that made the powerful nervous. And now he is dead. Executed like a common criminal between two thieves.

Peter, the one Jesus called his rock, denied three times that he even knew him. He is sitting somewhere right now in this story, carrying that weight. Mary Magdalene, who was among the most devoted, has no body to anoint because the tomb was sealed before the Sabbath began. Mary, his mother, has buried her son.

They are hiding. They are afraid the authorities will come for them next.

This is what Saturday feels like — the day when the story seems to be over and the ending is terrible.

The Church has a tradition on this night called the Easter Vigil, and it is one of the most ancient and beautiful rituals in Christianity. It begins in total darkness. A single fire is lit outside the church. From that fire, a large candle is lit — the Paschal candle — and carried inside. One flame in the dark. Then, person by person, the flame is passed until the whole church glows with candlelight.

And in that candlelight, they read. Not just the story of Jesus — they go all the way back to the beginning. They read from Genesis: "In the beginning, God created the heavens and the earth, and the earth was without form, and darkness was over the face of the deep." They read the story of Abraham, willing to sacrifice his son. They read from Exodus — the Israelites trapped at the Red Sea, the waters parting, an impossible path through the impossible.

They read from the prophet Isaiah: "All you who are thirsty, come to the water."

They read from Ezekiel, the vision of the valley of dry bones — an entire field of skeletons, and God asks, "Can these bones live?" And breath enters them, and they rise.

Do you see what the Church is doing? It is laying out the whole pattern. Darkness, then light. Slavery, then freedom. Death, then life. The entire arc of the story, from the first day of creation to this dark Saturday, is about one thing: that endings are not always what they seem.

But the people in the story do not know that yet. That is what makes this day so honest. Holy Saturday does not rush to the happy ending. It stays in the grief. It respects the doubt. It says: yes, this is real. The loss is real. The fear is real. The silence is real.

Francisco — you know from the stories you read that the hardest part of any great narrative is not the battle. It is the moment after defeat, when the hero does not yet know whether the story will turn. This is that moment.

Sebastian — think of it like a rest in music. Not empty space, but a silence that makes the next note mean something.

Tonight, all over the world, people will sit in the dark and wait. They will light one candle. They will listen to stories that are thousands of years old. And at some point in the night, the church will erupt — bells ringing, lights blazing, voices singing.

But not yet.

Right now, it is still Saturday. And we wait.
