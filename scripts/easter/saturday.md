# Holy Saturday

Francisco, Sebastian — today is Holy Saturday.

Yesterday, we heard the story of Good Friday. Jesus was arrested at night in a garden. He was tried, condemned, and crucified on a hill outside Jerusalem. His mother stood at the foot of the cross. A stone was rolled across the entrance to his tomb. And then — silence.

Today, we are still in that silence.

This is the strangest day of the year. Nothing happens. And that is the whole point.

The people who loved Jesus do not know what comes next. They only know he is gone. Think about that. They had left everything — jobs, families, homes — to follow someone they believed was sent by God. They watched him heal the sick, calm storms, speak with an authority that made powerful people nervous. And now he is dead. Executed like a criminal.

Peter — the one Jesus had called his rock, his most trusted friend — denied three times that he even knew Jesus. And now Peter has to live with that. Mary Magdalene, who had been among the most devoted, cannot even care for the body because the tomb was sealed before the Sabbath. And Mary, his mother, has buried her son.

They are hiding in locked rooms, afraid the authorities will come for them next.

This is what Saturday feels like. The story seems to be over, and the ending is terrible.

But tonight, something beautiful happens. The Church has a tradition called the Easter Vigil, and it is one of the oldest rituals in Christianity. It begins in total darkness. A single fire is lit outside the church. From that fire, a large candle is lit and carried inside. One flame in the dark. Then, person by person, the flame is shared — passed from candle to candle — until the whole church glows with light.

And in that candlelight, they read. They go all the way back to the beginning. They read from Genesis — how God created the heavens and the earth, and how the earth was without form, and darkness covered the deep. They read the story of Abraham. They read from Exodus — the Israelites trapped at the Red Sea, the waters parting, an impossible path opening through the impossible.

They read from the prophet Ezekiel — the vision of a valley filled with dry bones, and God asking, "Can these bones live?" And breath enters them, and they rise.

Do you hear the pattern? Darkness, then light. Slavery, then freedom. Death, then life. The whole story — from the first day of creation to this quiet Saturday — keeps pointing the same way. Endings are not always what they seem.

But the people in the story do not know that yet. That is what makes this day so honest. Holy Saturday does not skip ahead to the good part. It stays in the grief. It respects the doubt. It says: the loss is real. The fear is real. The silence is real.

Francisco — you know from the stories you read that the hardest part of any great narrative is not the battle. It is the moment after defeat, when the hero does not know if the story will turn. This is that moment.

Sebastian — think of it like a rest in music. Not empty space — but a silence that makes the next note mean something.

Tonight, all over the world, people will sit in the dark and wait. They will light one small flame. They will listen to stories that are thousands of years old. And at some point in the night, the church will erupt — bells ringing, lights blazing, voices singing.

But not yet.

Right now, it is still Saturday. And we wait.
