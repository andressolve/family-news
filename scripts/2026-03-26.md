## World and Politics

The US-Israel military campaign against Iran is now in its fourth week, and the picture is shifting. Iran has rejected President Trump's ceasefire proposal and put forward a counterproposal demanding safeguards against future attacks and sovereignty over the Strait of Hormuz. That strait is the key detail here. Roughly a fifth of the world's oil passes through it, and any prolonged disruption there affects everything from gas prices to grocery bills. Secretary of State Rubio is headed to France this week to make the case to skeptical G7 allies. Meanwhile, the IAEA says the US and Iran may hold broader talks this weekend in Islamabad. We'll see if diplomacy gains traction.

In Lebanon, the death toll from Israeli strikes has passed ten ninety since March second, and there are warnings of a possible ground incursion south of the Litani River. UN Secretary General Guterres called the conflict "out of control." In Colombia, investigators are still working through the wreckage of Monday's C-130 military transport crash that killed sixty-nine people on takeoff. President Petro blamed the US-donated aircraft's condition, while critics point to reduced military flight hours under his administration.

## Markets and Business

Markets are volatile. The S and P five hundred closed up half a percent yesterday at sixty-five ninety-one, with the Dow gaining three hundred five points. But futures are down seven-tenths of a percent this morning as uncertainty returns. A forty-eight-hour US pause on strikes against Iranian energy infrastructure is about to expire, and oil prices are climbing again on fears of a broader escalation.

A modest softening, but investors are reading it as a potential signal the Fed may revisit its rate stance. In tech, Nvidia continues trading near record levels around one hundred eighty dollars a share. ## Technology

March has been one of the busiest months in AI history. OpenAI launched GPT five-point-four earlier this month with context windows over a million tokens. China is having its own AI moment, with five major models released this month from Alibaba, Tencent, Baidu, and ByteDance. Alibaba's Qwen three-point-five runs on laptops and phones, and its nine-billion-parameter version is outperforming models fifty times its size.

Apple announced a completely rebuilt Siri powered by Google's Gemini model, arriving with iOS twenty-six-point-four. And at Nvidia's GTC conference, the standout was OpenClaw, an open-source AI agent framework that lets autonomous agents run locally on personal computers. Jensen Huang called it the next ChatGPT. Whether that's hype or substance, local AI agents are clearly the direction things are moving.
