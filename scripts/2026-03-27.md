It's Friday, March twenty-seventh, twenty twenty-six. Good morning. Here's what's happening today.

## World and Politics

The Iran conflict enters day twenty-seven with no ceasefire in sight. President Trump extended the deadline for strikes on Iran's power grid to April sixth, saying talks are ongoing. But Iran has formally rejected the US fifteen-point peace proposal and submitted its own conditions, including recognition of Tehran's authority over the Strait of Hormuz. That strait remains nearly shut down. Before the war, a hundred thirty ships passed through daily. Now it's fewer than six. Iran's parliament is moving to formalize transit fees for any ship that does pass through. The Pentagon is deploying up to three thousand paratroopers from the Eighty-Second Airborne to the region, plus thousands of Marines.

In other news, Nepal is swearing in a new prime minister today. Balendra Shah, a rapper-turned-politician, won in a landslide after last year's youth-led protests. And the UN General Assembly adopted a resolution declaring the transatlantic slave trade the gravest crime against humanity and calling for reparations.

## Markets and Business

Rough day on Wall Street yesterday. The Dow fell four hundred sixty-nine points. The S and P five hundred dropped one-point-seven percent. And the Nasdaq tumbled two-point-four percent, officially entering correction territory, down more than ten percent from its high. Futures are slightly lower again this morning.

The driver is oil. Brent crude jumped nearly six percent to a hundred eight dollars a barrel. West Texas Intermediate climbed to ninety-four dollars. With the Strait of Hormuz effectively closed, energy prices are feeding directly into inflation fears and pushing Treasury yields higher. The ten-year yield is creeping toward five percent, which is the number that really spooks equity investors.

Tech got hammered. Meta fell nearly eight percent after child safety court rulings and more layoffs. Micron is down twenty-two percent in six trading days. Nvidia dropped four percent. The bright spots were energy producers and Clear Secure, which rallied as TSA airport delays hit four-hour waits at some US airports.

## Technology

Apple is making a major platform move. Bloomberg reports that iOS twenty-seven will open Siri to competing AI assistants beyond just ChatGPT. This would let users choose their preferred AI service directly through Siri, turning the iPhone into a more open AI platform. It's a significant shift from Apple's historically closed ecosystem.

Meanwhile, Trump is forming a twenty-four-member AI policy council, co-chaired by David Sacks and including Mark Zuckerberg, Larry Ellison, and Jensen Huang. The council will advise on AI regulation and industrial policy.

Meta cut about seven hundred jobs this week, mostly in Reality Labs and recruiting, as it doubles down on generative AI investment. And OpenAI launched a safety bug bounty program focused specifically on identifying AI abuse risks across its products.

## Francisco and Sebastian's Corner

Francisco and Sebastian, Xbox held a Partner Preview event yesterday with some interesting reveals. The big ones were new looks at STALKER two, The Expanse Osiris Reborn, and Stranger Than Heaven. Several titles are coming to Game Pass.

## One More Thing

The International Olympic Committee announced that starting with the twenty twenty-eight Los Angeles Games, all athletes competing in women's events will be required to undergo genetic testing. It's one of the most significant policy changes in Olympic history and is already generating intense debate about science, fairness, and privacy in sport.
