It's Monday, April fourteen, twenty twenty-six. Good morning. Here's what's happening today.

## World and Politics

Hungary woke up to a new political era this morning. Peter Magyar, a former Orban loyalist turned opposition leader, won a landslide victory on Sunday, ending Viktor Orban's sixteen-year hold on power. Voter turnout was the highest since the nineteen nineties. It's one of the most significant political shifts in Europe in years, and a sign that even deeply entrenched leaders can be unseated when enough people show up.

On the Middle East, the US military blockade on Iranian ports is now in effect, but there are hints of a path forward. President Trump signaled a willingness to resume talks with Iran, which helped calm markets overnight. China's Foreign Minister Wang Yi called on the international community to preserve what he called the extremely fragile ceasefire. Meanwhile, Russia's Foreign Minister Lavrov is in Beijing today for talks with Chinese officials, a meeting that will almost certainly touch on Iran and the broader geopolitical picture.

Elsewhere, a stampede at a mountaintop fortress in northern Haiti killed at least twenty-five people over the weekend. And Peru's presidential election is still unresolved after ballot delivery failures forced authorities to extend voting by a day.

## Markets and Business

Stocks are set to open higher this morning. Futures are up after Trump's signal that he's open to resuming Iran talks, and the S and P five hundred has now fully erased its losses since the Iran conflict began. Oil prices are easing on the prospect of renewed diplomacy.

Earnings season is officially underway. Goldman Sachs reported better-than-expected first-quarter results this morning. Johnson and Johnson also beat estimates, posting revenue of twenty-four point one billion dollars, up ten percent year over year, and raised its full-year guidance. Later this week, we'll hear from JPMorgan Chase, Citigroup, Wells Fargo, Morgan Stanley, and Bank of America. These bank earnings will be the first real window into how the Iran conflict and oil shock have affected the financial system.

In deal news, reports surfaced that United Airlines CEO Scott Kirby proposed a merger with American Airlines to senior officials back in February. American Airlines stock jumped four percent on the news.

## Technology

The Stanford AI Index for twenty twenty-six is out, and it paints a clear picture of where the industry stands. The top models, including Anthropic's Claude and Google's Gemini three point one Pro, now score above fifty percent on the Humanity's Last Exam benchmark, a test designed to be extremely difficult. But a study published in Nature found that human scientists still outperform the best AI agents on complex research tasks. The gap is closing, but it hasn't closed.

Meta's AI spending plans are staggering. The company said its AI-related capital expenditures in twenty twenty-six will be between one hundred fifteen billion and one hundred thirty-five billion dollars, nearly double last year. That's the scale of investment now required to stay competitive in frontier AI.

NVIDIA used National Robotics Week to showcase its full-stack workflow for AI-powered robots, connecting simulation, robot learning, and edge computing in a single pipeline. Physical AI is moving from demos to deployable systems.

## One More Thing

Here's a thought from the Hungary election. Peter Magyar was an insider, a loyalist in Orban's own party, before he broke ranks and challenged the system from within. Sometimes the person who changes things isn't the outsider. It's the one who knows the machine well enough to take it apart.
