It's Saturday, April fourth, twenty twenty-six. Good morning. Here's what's happening today.

## World and Politics

The war with Iran is now entering its sixth week. Yesterday, two U.S. combat aircraft were lost — an F-fifteen fighter jet shot down over Iranian territory and an A-ten attack plane that crashed near the Strait of Hormuz. The search continues for a missing American pilot who ejected over Iran. Two rescue helicopters were also hit, though their crews made it back to base. Meanwhile, Israel has launched a ground invasion into Lebanon, targeting Hezbollah fighters who have been firing rockets into Israel in support of Iran. Over a million Lebanese civilians have been displaced and more than thirteen hundred killed, according to Lebanon's government. Britain is leading diplomatic talks with about forty countries to try to reopen the Strait of Hormuz, where Iran's blockade has choked off roughly twenty percent of the world's oil and gas shipments. On a brighter note, it has been three months since Venezuelan President Nicolas Maduro was captured, and ordinary Venezuelans say they can now openly discuss politics and demonstrate in the streets for the first time in years.

## Markets and Business

Markets are closed today for Good Friday, so no trading action. But it was a strong week overall. The S and P five hundred rose three point four percent, the Dow gained nearly three percent, and the Nasdaq surged four point four percent, helped by a strong March jobs report. The U.S. economy added a hundred and seventy-eight thousand jobs, beating expectations, and unemployment dipped to four point three percent. Oil remains the big concern. U.S. crude is sitting at a hundred and eleven dollars and fifty-four cents a barrel, more than double pre-conflict levels, as the Strait of Hormuz blockade continues. That is hammering airlines especially — the global airline industry had forecast forty-one billion dollars in profits this year, but jet fuel costs are forcing carriers to scramble. Tesla shares fell more than five percent after reporting that first-quarter deliveries rose year over year but slipped from the prior quarter. And Globalstar jumped thirteen percent on reports that Amazon may be considering an acquisition.

## Technology

OpenAI says it has surpassed twenty-five billion dollars in annualized revenue and is taking early steps toward a public listing, potentially as soon as late this year. Microsoft announced a ten billion dollar investment in Japan over the next three years to expand AI infrastructure and deepen cybersecurity cooperation with the Japanese government. On the regulatory side, California Governor Gavin Newsom ordered state agencies to develop recommendations for AI contract standards addressing harms like civil rights violations and unlawful surveillance. Yahoo is re-entering the search market with Scout, an AI-powered answer engine built on Anthropic's technology. And Cisco unveiled a new zero trust architecture designed specifically to secure autonomous AI agents and multi-agent systems, a sign of how quickly the industry is moving toward deploying AI that acts on its own.

## Francisco and Sebastian's Corner

Francisco, Sebastian — big space news this week. NASA launched Artemis two on Wednesday, sending four astronauts on the first crewed flight around the Moon in over fifty years. On April sixth, the crew will reach about two hundred and fifty-three thousand miles from Earth, breaking the distance record set by Apollo thirteen back in nineteen seventy. Also yesterday, an Atlas five rocket launched twenty-nine Amazon internet satellites, the heaviest payload that rocket has ever carried.

On the gaming front, if you have Amazon Prime, XCOM Enemy Unknown Complete Pack is free starting this week. It is one of the best turn-based tactical combat games ever made — you command a squad defending Earth against an alien invasion, and every decision matters because if a soldier dies, they are gone for good. April is shaping up as a huge month for strategy games. And Hades two, the sequel to Supergiant's action roguelike, launches on PlayStation five and Xbox on April fourteenth.

## One More Thing

Scientists using the new Vera Rubin Observatory have already discovered over eleven thousand new asteroids from just its preliminary data. That is more asteroids found faster than any telescope in history. The observatory is just getting started, and astronomers expect it to eventually catalog millions of objects in our solar system that we never knew were there.
