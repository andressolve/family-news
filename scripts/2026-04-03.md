It's Friday, April third, twenty twenty-six. Good morning. Here's what's happening today.

## World and Politics

The Iran war is in its thirty-fifth day and there's no off-ramp in sight. Trump's primetime address on Tuesday promised the conflict would wrap up in two to three weeks, but he also vowed to hit Iran extremely hard and threatened to destroy all of Iran's power plants and oil infrastructure. The speech sent oil prices surging and did nothing to calm markets or allies.

The UN Security Council voted yesterday on a resolution to authorize a multinational naval force to reopen the Strait of Hormuz. Russia and China vetoed it. The strait remains effectively closed. Kuwait International Airport is still shut down after drone strikes damaged radar and fuel systems. Dubai's airport is operating on a reduced schedule. The conflict's ripple effects now extend well beyond Iran's borders.

The death toll stands at over two thousand seventy-six in Iran, at least twenty-four in Israel, thirteen US service members, and twenty-seven killed across Gulf states. Airline fuel costs have more than doubled, putting enormous pressure on carriers worldwide.

On a brighter note, Artemis Two is on its way to the Moon. After launching Wednesday evening, the crew successfully completed the critical translunar injection burn yesterday, breaking free of Earth orbit. The four astronauts are now on their way to a lunar flyby on Monday. Today they're practicing medical procedures in zero gravity and rehearsing moon photography.

## Markets and Business

US markets are closed today for Good Friday. Regular trading resumes Monday.

Before the break, the March jobs report came in stronger than expected. The US added a hundred seventy-eight thousand jobs, beating all estimates. The two-year Treasury yield rose four basis points to three-point-eight-four percent on the news. S and P five hundred futures slipped slightly.

Stepping back, the S and P five hundred did manage its first weekly gain since the war began. But the bigger picture is still difficult. Oil remains above a hundred dollars a barrel. The Strait of Hormuz blockade is the largest oil supply disruption in history. Gas at the pump in the US has topped four dollars a gallon.

Tesla reported first-quarter deliveries that rose year over year but fell from the prior quarter. Shares dropped more than five percent. Intel jumped nine percent after announcing it's buying back a forty-nine percent stake in its Irish fabrication facility from Apollo for fourteen-point-two billion dollars. And Globalstar surged thirteen percent on reports Amazon may be considering an acquisition.

## Technology

A supply chain attack hit the AI ecosystem this week. An open-source AI tooling library called LiteLLM was exploited to target Mercor, an AI startup. The attack demonstrated how widely used open-source components in the AI stack can serve as vectors for sophisticated intrusions. This is likely to accelerate the push for AI-specific security audits and SOC two compliance for model infrastructure.

OpenAI acquired the tech podcast TBPN, expanding into the creator economy. And Cognichip raised sixty million dollars to build AI-powered tools for semiconductor design. Their pitch is using machine intelligence to cut the cost and time of developing advanced chips.

Nvidia announced a partnership with Alpamayo, an AI simulation specialist, to build hyper-realistic testing environments for autonomous vehicles using Nvidia's DRIVE Orin and Thor platforms.

## One More Thing

A new AI legislative tracker shows that forty-seven US states now have active AI bills in their legislatures, up from just twelve a year ago. The patchwork of state-level regulation is growing faster than any federal framework. For anyone building or using AI tools, the compliance landscape is about to get a lot more complicated.
